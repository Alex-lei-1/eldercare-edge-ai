Real-time fall detection & a local voice companion

An elder-care system that runs entirely on an Orange Pi 5 Plus (RK3588). A 6-TOPS NPU drives pose-based fall detection; a fully offline voice assistant checks on the elder and alerts family — all without sending a single frame or word to the cloud.

What it does

Two AI workloads share one low-power board and one camera + mic.

Vision

Pose-based fall detection

A YOLOv8n-pose model on the NPU streams 17 keypoints per person. A temporal state machine (NORMAL → WARNING → LYING → FALL_CONFIRMED) judges body angle, floor height and aspect ratio over time, then confirms a fall and fires an alert with a snapshot.

Voice

Fully offline voice companion

Wake word → on-device ASR → a local LLM → neural TTS, with acoustic echo cancellation so the assistant never talks to itself. After a fall it asks "are you OK?" and lets the elder barge in with "I'm fine" / "help" to decide whether to escalate.

End-to-end architecture

Camera & ISP → NPU inference → three outputs → fall logic → message bus → family alert & voice, plus a parallel voice-companion pipeline and a systemd ops layer.

The fall-alarm path, step by step

From photons to a family notification — every stage runs on the board.

  1. Capture. ov13855 → RKAIQ ISP (auto-exposure, 60 Hz anti-flicker, low-light tuning) → V4L2 NV12 DMA-BUF with an 8-buffer pool.
  2. Infer. Capture thread (A55) hands frames to 3 parallel RKNN contexts (A76 + NPU). Each runs RGA NV12→RGB then YOLOv8n-pose 640, post-process and tracking — ~15 fps with zero dropped frames.
  3. Fan-out. Keypoints over UDP, an AI-overlay H264 stream over RTSP (hardware MPP encoder, multi-client), and a hardware-JPEG ring in tmpfs for event evidence.
  4. Decide. The fall service feeds keypoints into a temporal state machine that confirms a fall from body angle, floor height and aspect ratio sustained over time.
  5. Notify. On FALL_CONFIRMED it publishes to MQTT; the alert service pushes the family (ntfy) with a snapshot pulled from the JPEG ring.
  6. Check in. The voice service speaks "are you OK?" without blocking, lets the elder barge in, subtracts the prompt's own echo from the transcript, and classifies ok vs sos to reassure or escalate.

Engineering highlights

The interesting decisions — the ones that took measurement, not guessing.

640 model at 384 speed

The fast 384 model missed lying/distant poses (≈81% vs 100% for 640). Instead of trading accuracy, three parallel RKNN contexts restored the 640 model to ~15 fps — accuracy and throughput.

The zero-copy trap

The "obvious" full rknn_set_io_mem zero-copy path was measured 2× slower for the 640 model. Keeping RGA pre-process + rknn_inputs_set won — a reminder to benchmark, not assume.

Industrial low-light ISP

Rather than software brightening, the RK3588 hardware ISP (RKAIQ) is tuned at the IQ level — exposure curve, gain, and 60 Hz anti-flicker — so the auto-exposure adapts to the dark scene and stays artifact-free.

Hearing past itself

A full-duplex PipeWire helper runs speex AEC plus an echo-envelope gate so the assistant doesn't transcribe its own TTS, while still letting a real person barge in over the speaker.

Reliable RTSP

The live stream uses a per-client GStreamer media/appsrc fan-out with independent timestamps, so viewers can reconnect and multiple phones can watch at once.

Verified, not assumed

The deployed C++ fall logic is a line-by-line port of the Python reference and was proven byte-identical on a 35-clip replay corpus — the eval scores describe the code that actually ships.
